The Desire to Do Something for Myself

A Life Built Step by Step

Gijsbertus “Gil” Kommer

Recorded and edited by Robin Kommer

Preface

by Robin Kommer

Dad once said, ‘Isn’t the memory phenomenal? You close your eyes and you see yourself — in the past — there you are.’

As his daughter, I often wondered what would happen for future generations when all the fabulous stories shared and enjoyed by our family today had become long-gone memories. Our great-grandchildren, and the many generations still to come, should not miss out on the amazing tales of their Opa Kommer.

So we are documenting and recording Dad’s remarkable journey through life.

One of Dad’s memorable sayings is: ‘I’ve got no problems with that.’

I had no problems secretly recording Dad for around two years and typing up his tales. Then one day I sent him a brief sample of around ten thousand words. He read it, then quietly looked at me and remarked on my extraordinary memory in recalling his stories so well.

That gave me a laugh.

I said, ‘Dad, when was the last time you heard me tell a story in such perfect detail?’

He looked at me questioningly.

Then I produced the recordings, and for the first time he heard his own voice telling those stories. Dad sat in his chair, crossed his arms across his chest, and remained silent.

Finally, he looked at me sideways and said, ‘Well, I’d better be careful what I say to you in the future!’

It took nearly a year before Dad came to the recording party, but once he did, he became an active participant in sharing his life stories, knowing full well that I would be recording every word.

Chapter 1 — Gil’s Story

Life Is an Adventure

I’ve had so many adventures in my life and have certainly achieved a lot. I’ve also built a lot of buildings. It’s been, and still is, a very full life — with tragedies along the way.

Whether it’s losing Helen or Uncle Dick, I feel that as long as you keep living the life you’re living, you can carry them in your heart. You think of your loved ones, but you don’t allow your life to become totally different because they’re gone.

When Helen passed away, it was the saddest moment in my life. But we still had young Peter, Robbie, Bram and Annelies — I had a family. Yes, we were sad. It was a tragedy.

I still look at the photograph of Helen, and the funny thing is I’m looking at a young girl’s photograph. I’m not looking at a lady who would be fifty-two now, with a couple of kids and all.

There’s one thing I think would be silly, and that would be to have regrets — to say I would have done things differently. Regret is a very negative feeling.

If I regretted marrying the mother of my children, Ruth, then none of my children would be here — not Bram or Robbie. I wouldn’t have adopted Jennifer, and I wouldn’t have had Helen.

I can’t say that I regret. These were the decisions I made at the time. What happens when you start regretting things? Those regrets stay with you. They keep coming back into your mind.

Regrets can’t be talking points with people — you’d be bloody boring if all you spoke about was your regrets. The fact remains, if you had made one single different decision in your life, then your whole life would have been different.

Flip of a Coin

Back in New Zealand, Uncle Piet and I had to decide which one of us would go back to Holland to celebrate Oma and Opa’s twenty-fifth wedding anniversary.

At the time, Uncle Piet said, “You should go, because you’ve been here first and you’re the oldest.”

“Piet, let’s toss for it,” I replied.

We tossed the coin, and Uncle Piet went back to Holland — and his whole life changed.

My whole life would have changed if I had gone back to Holland. Look at what would have happened if I had won the toss. I would have gone back, and the army would have grabbed me. I would have stayed there, and I wouldn’t have met Ruth — your mum.

A thousand and one things could have happened. My whole life could have changed with the simple toss of a coin.

At one point, we considered moving our business to South Africa. When I saw the way South Africa was heading after the Sharpeville massacre, I decided no — South Africa was not for me.

Maybe it could have worked for me personally, but it certainly wouldn’t have worked for my children or grandchildren.

So that was another decision that changed my life — leaving New Zealand, selling up, and moving back to Holland instead of going to South Africa.

Another major decision.

When you regret a decision, you wouldn’t be in the position you are in today. You would have had a totally different life if you had taken the other path.

So there’s no use regretting anything. You can’t suddenly say, “I would have liked things to have been different,” because if things had been different at that point, your whole life would have changed.

Chapter 2 — Growing Up in the Netherlands

Family History

Granddad had two brothers and two sisters. Om Jouk had two children, Geder and Matti, who now live in America. Om Derek has one daughter left, also called Matti, who is married with children. Then there was Opa, Tante Nel, who had two children, Om Ven and Klaus, and Tante Mattie, who had three — Ewout, Marilel and Els.

So I’ve still got eight cousins.

They had another boy before Opa, and he passed away as an infant. He was also called Piet.

There was a grave for that little infant at the cemetery, and Om Jan and Opa had promised their mother — my grandmother — that they would look after the grave for as long as they lived.

At one point, the family received a notice asking what was to be done with the grave, because in Holland you have to pay a yearly fee to maintain it.

That’s how it works in Holland — it’s not like Australia.

In the end, we decided not to continue with it, as it had been a promise made by Om Jan and my father for their little brother.

Mum had a similar situation with her grandfather, Parke Jouke. Nobody liked him. Om Jouke received a phone call in Holland asking who was going to maintain that grave. He rang Mum one night and asked, “Are you going to pay for the grave?”

Mum said, “Not me — I never liked him.”

He replied, “Good on you, I didn’t like him either.”

What happens then is that the grave is eventually cleared. The remains are removed, cremated, and the name disappears.

There is, however, a heritage grave in a very old part of town for Mum’s grandmother. She lived to 103 and is buried on church grounds with her husband. That land is not council ground.

In Holland, names carry through. A woman keeps her maiden name attached to her married name.

Our Kommer family in Holland traces back to Berkhout. In Alkmaar there was Om Jan, Geder and Matti. We were born on the Hofplein. In Broek op Langedijk was Om Derek, and in Heerhugowaard lived Tante Nel and Tante Mattie for a time.

The Beginning

I was born Gijsbertus Kommer on 28 March 1931 to my parents, Joanne Helen Kommer and Peter Kommer.

I was one of four sons — the eldest, Bram Kommer, then Peter Kommer, myself, and Derek Kommer.

Town Life

It’s always interesting talking about the history of a town, and how much things have changed in the local villages over time.

Back then, towns had many tradespeople. Basket and cane weavers were very common. We had the baker and the cobbler. Steel wasn’t widely used for everyday purposes, so the cobblers and blacksmiths worked on all the equipment for horses and carts.

There was a fellow who sold knives and scissors, but he also sharpened them. He had a specialty shop for sharpening shears used for sheep. Another trade was the wool comber at the shearing sheds.

The kaasboer — the cheese maker — lived only a few houses down from us in Alkmaar, opposite the Waagtoren church. He made a type of cheese similar to what you’d call Kraft cheese today.

One tradition that has lasted the test of time is the brass band. I think every village has one. Alkmaar actually had four — the city brass band, and even the Catholics had their own.

Bolsward, where Mum lived, was a small city of around ten thousand people, and it had three brass bands.

Dutch lollies were an absolute favourite in our family. As kids, we loved a “magic ball”, which we could buy for one cent. It changed colours as you sucked it, which is why we called it magic.

Another was the dauwdrop, a very soft liquorice. You pressed it with your thumb, and then you had this lump of liquorice sitting on your thumb. So we’d walk around sucking our thumbs with it — a bit stupid, really.

Chapter 3 — Family Life

Waking Up with Ice Sheets

Waking up with ice on our blankets was a regular thing. The vapour from our breath would freeze on top of the blankets. It could be ten to fifteen degrees of frost outside, and we were sleeping upstairs with absolutely no heating.

The bedrooms would eventually reach extreme cold, yet we were still warm in bed under thick woollen blankets. There was no central heating in those days. Even the water in the sink would have ice on it.

We kept a small pot under the bed so we didn’t have to go downstairs in the middle of the night in the freezing cold.

We were on a sewage system with a large tank in the backyard, similar to a bio-cycle system. Every couple of years, a special truck would come and empty it.

Farmers had outhouses with just a simple hole. Not like the Australian ones with deep drops — just a basic setup.

Homemade Underwear

Oma knitted underwear for all the children, along with the high woollen socks we wore while walking or riding our bikes to school in the snow.

My Parents

My father was born in 1906. He left school at the age of twelve, in 1918, just after the First World War. He was a hard worker.

At thirteen, he left home to work on a local dairy farm. That was quite normal in those days — once you finished primary school, you went out to work.

Dairy farming is hard work, seven days a week, starting very early each morning. It was often easier to live with the farmer and his family.

My grandparents lived in a small house in Ber…, and Piet P, the farmer, lived about half a kilometre up the road. So my father was still close to home.

Piet became a lifelong friend of Dad’s. He had a daughter named Nel. Later, after Piet passed away, Nel married a doctor, Hank K., and they lived in Bereka near Alkmaar. The friendship between the families continued for many years.

Dad developed a strong interest in cattle and built a close friendship with a local vet, who trained him as a veterinary midwife. He helped cows during difficult births and, when necessary, saved the cow if a calf had died inside.

It was tough work, but cattle were a livelihood. If you had thirty or forty cows in those days, you were considered a wealthy farmer. You also had horses, pigs, and calves for the following year — everything needed to be housed through winter.

Winters back then were much harsher than they are today.

My mother was also born in 1906. She studied at college and became a schoolteacher in a small town north of Bravaand, called Peel.

That’s where Mum and Dad met. Their relationship began in Peel, and they were married there on 6 June 1929.

While travelling between farms to help with cattle, Dad realised there was a shortage of simple medicines and ointments for animals.

At the time, medicines could not easily be registered unless developed with proper pharmaceutical training. The nearest college teaching pharmacy was in the city of Horan, about twenty-three kilometres from Alkmaar.

Mum enrolled there and rode her pushbike five days a week through all kinds of weather to study. She even continued cycling while pregnant with her fourth son, Oom Derek. With a strong headwind, you’d be lucky to manage five kilometres an hour. The bikes had no gears.

Dad was also travelling constantly by pushbike, building his business among the farmers.

We were looked after by a housemaid, a local girl named Cret, who was about seventeen at the time. To us, she seemed quite old. It was common for farmers’ daughters to take live-in domestic work so they could earn a wage and have somewhere to live.

Cret stayed with us until she married. We also knew her father well — a cheerful man with a big head of white curly hair and one lazy eye. He used to make flutes or whistles for us out of long reeds.

Mum’s pharmacy business in Alkmaar developed many medicines, particularly for farm animals, including a black drawing ointment.

Over time, Mum and Dad built up the business. They had agents in England, Berlin, and Copenhagen, and developed a small factory called Komico (Kommer & Company), producing medicines for livestock.

Our bathroom even became a small laboratory where Mum created her formulas. Everything was professionally packaged and sold through agents. She never patented her formulas.

Mum later gave me a book containing all her recipes and discoveries. I never lost it — I just don’t know where it is at the moment.

Dad was a bold businessman, despite having very little formal education.

At one stage, a serious chicken disease spread across farms, killing thousands. A research laboratory owned by Jacquar in Genbloux in Belgium developed a vaccine.

Three companies in Holland competed to market it. Dad’s business was the smallest. They were given a few months to prove who could develop the market the fastest.

Dad went all out. His advertising was extensive — newspapers, posters — he was brilliant. He won the contract ahead of the larger companies.

It was a major turning point in the business.

Later, there were issues with their London agency, and money stopped coming back to Holland. Dad sent the accountant, Klaus De F., to investigate.

He arrived in London around 8 May 1940. Two days later, Germany invaded Holland. He didn’t return for five years.

The London office was eventually bombed, and Dad never recovered the money.

By the end of the war, the business had collapsed, and he had to start again from nothing.

But Dad had energy and a strong entrepreneurial mind. Even in retirement in Australia, he was always developing new ideas — from Van Diemen’s Gifts at the factory to planting garlic.

The company Komico still exists in Holland today, producing some of the original ointments Mum developed.

My Grandparents

On Dad’s side, both his parents were orphans.

Both my grandfathers — from Mum’s and Dad’s sides — were house painters.

When insurance became more common, allowing people to insure farms, homes, and livestock, my grandfather Opa Kommer took over an insurance agency and became a well-known agent in the local town.

By then, my father’s parents had moved to Alkmaar. I visited them often as a child. They lived only about two hundred metres from our home.

My mother’s parents lived in Varaswen. Opa and Oma Van der Zwaard — about eighty kilometres from Alkmaar.

Chapter 4 — Youthful Adventures

Falling into the Canal

When I was young, a big part of my life was living opposite the Alkmaar Kaasmarkt (cheese market) and the big church, the Waagtoren, on the Waagplein. I lived there from about four to fifteen or sixteen, leaving straight after the war.

I was about four or five years old when I fell into the canal near our home in Alkmaar. People later told me they saw my clogs floating. I was just a child riding my scooter around. I wanted to make a big right-hand turn towards home and, as I turned, I rode straight into the canal.

They said the clogs came up first because they were timber. I remember sinking under the water. When I opened my eyes, I saw all this green water, so I held my breath and then sort of floated back up again. Luckily, I surfaced behind one of the boats, not underneath it. I hung onto the rudder.

When I looked up, I saw all these stupid faces looking at me — all the farmers.

At the time, a Dutchman called Jan Vas Spadol, who was studying for the priesthood, lived across the street from the canal. He happened to be at his desk, looked out of the second-floor window, and saw me fall in.

Apparently, he rushed down the stairs, across the road, climbed onto the boat, and pulled me out by the arm. I didn’t like that much — it hurt. He just heaved me up like that.

I went into the house, and Mum — your grandmother — was shocked to pieces. She dried me off while I stood in the kitchen.

I remember crying and asking Mum, ‘Will I die now?’

That’s when she decided, ‘That’s it — you’re all having swimming lessons.’ So the three of us — Peter, Bram and I — were sent off to learn.

I was about seven when we did our swimming test. The water was very clear, and they had this wooden body at the bottom of the pool. You had to dive in, bring it up, then swim with one arm while holding its chin with the other, and get it safely to the side. There were two parts to the lessons — one to swim, the other to rescue.

School

You weren’t allowed to use the cane when I was growing up — it had mostly been phased out in Holland. I did see one boy hit with a ruler. He had to open his hand, but it was more of a token discipline.

Your mum, though, who lived in Friesland, saw boys being hit with a big wooden stick — at least a metre long. They had to bend over and were hit on the backside. Some of them were big boys, sixteen or eighteen.

I’m naturally left-handed, but I had to write with my right. Your mum’s father was the same. Whenever I picked up the pen with my left hand, the teacher would quietly move it to my right. In those days, being left-handed was seen as not quite right — or even a bit evil.

I left school at seventeen or eighteen. I was very good at arithmetic — we did complex sums, no calculators. My weakness was French.

Dance Classes

My teacher was Ms Prada. Mum wanted us to socialise more — we were four boys and no sisters — so she arranged dance classes with Tante Janny B, who was a seamstress.

There were the four of us. Bram danced with Janny B, and I danced with Neil DV, a friend of Janny’s and the daughter of a butcher.

I could never dance the waltz. I always got my feet mixed up trying to turn. Bloody awful dance for me. That was the first one we learned — and I still can’t do it.

Ice Hopping

We did some dangerous things in our youth. Winters were far more severe back then. Icebreakers would go through the canals, smashing the ice so ships could pass, leaving behind big floating blocks.

We used to ‘Scotch-a-lopen’ — like hopscotch — jumping from one block to another. ‘Scotch’ meant a block of ice, and ‘lopen’ meant walking.

When you stood on a small one, it would sink under your feet, so you had to jump quickly to the next. If you fell in, you were in real trouble — you couldn’t climb back onto the ice easily.

Some blocks sank the moment you stepped on them, so you aimed for the bigger ones — bop, bop, bop — and then you’d be cheering.

I think it was around 1944. There were about four of us when all of a sudden there was a shout from the bank — one of the fathers had seen his son doing it.

The boy was standing on a big block, scared to go back because he knew he’d get a hiding.

His father was yelling, ‘Come back here! How dare you!’

Half the time, parents didn’t even know what their kids were up to.

Ice Skating Dangerously

The most dangerous thing I ever did on skates was on the mead. Our skates were simple, but the steel had to be ground properly — left and right foot — so you could turn and push off the ice.

I was never a great skater — my ankles were weak — but I loved it.

There was a big bridge with arches underneath, where water flowed through. In the severe winter of 1944–1945, the ice there froze over.

The dare was to skate fast, then crouch low — because you couldn’t stand — and glide through under the bridge to the other side.

As you went through, you could hear the ice moving — not quite cracking, but shifting — making that high-pitched sound, like ‘eeeeee’.

We had to get a hell of a speed up, then drop onto our haunches to get through because you couldn’t stand under the bridge.

I never went back the other way.

Chapter 5 — Second World War

German Occupation

During the war, our way of life was surrounded by rumours. The liveliest part of every conversation was always the rumours.

We managed to procure a radio, which was illegal during the war. It was a new radio back in 1938–1939, a German Blaupunkt.

Right throughout the war, we were able to listen to the BBC in secret. If we had been discovered, there would have been a massive penalty. Not even our neighbours knew we had it.

The Germans controlled the news through a cable network system. Anyone with a shortwave radio had to hand it in. Most people relied on the three controlled stations — that’s how information was managed.

I remember hearing about the defeat of the Germans at El Alamein. Mum was incredibly happy.

She said, ‘Those so-and-so’s have been defeated!’

During the war, Opa kept rabbits and cows to help feed the family. He had access to a small polder just outside Alkmaar called the Huisvaderpolder.

He had become friendly with farmers there, including Piet H. and his German wife Muider Malansha.

Opa rented a small piece of land and kept cows. Later, when he had a factory, he employed Piet H. Dad was very loyal to people who were loyal to him.

He wasn’t a brutal man, but he made us work. Everyone had to pull their weight.

Hiding Dad in the Attic

Our house was very old, built around the 1500s. Over time, parts had been closed off, and we discovered hidden spaces.

There was a trapdoor hidden in a cupboard where you could climb into a concealed area. There was another hiding place on the second floor.

We only used them when raids — Razzias — were expected.

Sometimes we had warning. Even one of the local boys in the German Youth passed on information.

Dad would hide there, making sausages quietly. Once he even had a drink and started singing, and Mum had to tell him to be quiet.

Hiding Boys from the Germans

Mum and Dad hid young men so they wouldn’t be taken by the Germans.

We hid Piet V and another young man. To keep them busy, Dad gave them jobs — like chopping massive tree roots into kindling.

It was hard work, but it kept them occupied.

Many young men were taken to Germany as forced labour. One friend of mine was taken and later escaped after bombing raids. It took him three months to walk back home.

Blackouts

One day, while we were listening to the radio, there was a knock at the door. We panicked and hid it quickly.

It turned out to be a Dutch policeman warning us about light showing from the house.

After sunset, strict blackout rules applied. No light could be visible — everything was completely dark.

Towards the end of the war, we had no electricity and used candles and kerosene lamps.

Chapter 6 — Post War

Jubilation

The actual point of liberation after the war was when the Americans came into Paris. The absolute jubilation those people felt — knowing they were free, that the tyranny was gone — was incredible.

It was like a massive spring that had been under high tension all that time during the war, and then all of a sudden it was released.

When the Canadians came rumbling through our city, Alkmaar in Holland, you knew you were free. The joy and the dancing lasted for a full three days. It never stopped, and there was no liquor to drink — it was just pure, unadulterated joy of being free.

No more soldiers on the streets with guns. The tyranny was gone.

When the Americans went into Rome, Italy surrendered. All of a sudden, the Italians became the enemies of the Germans, whereas before the surrender they had been friends with them.

In Alkmaar we had the Whit Kruis (White Cross), the Red Cross and the Green Cross.

The White Cross was mainly a disinfectant point because a lot of people before the war had problems with lice and all those sorts of things — you know, the gypsies and all.

The White Cross station used to disinfect clothes as well as people. They had big steamers to disinfect all the clothes and blankets.

There was a massive number of Italians coming in at that time. Some of them wore those funny hats — they were Alpine people.

They all went through the White Cross stations. I had a friend whose father was a manager there, and he told me that, to some extent, many of the Italians were prisoners of war. The Germans had to get them through the disinfectant section first before doing anything else with them — that’s how bad the lice situation was.

All their clothes were taken off, they were given other clothing, and their old clothes were put into the steamers.

England 1948

After the war, in 1948, I went to England for a couple of weeks. I was only a youngster — seventeen years old. It was during our summer holidays in June or July.

I took a boat from Amanda to Harwich. I’ve never been so freezing in all my life.

It was an old passenger boat, and I managed to find a spot underneath a staircase. I can still remember it — there was a coil of rope under the stairs. I got myself onto the rope like it was a seat and just sat there, feeling bloody miserable.

I didn’t want to get up until we got to Harwich. I just sat there and said to myself, ‘Well, I’ll just sit here until we bloody arrive.’

It was a four-to-five-hour journey across to Harwich. From there I went to Bath.

During the war, Holland had five years of German occupation. England had also been fighting. I stayed at Clifford W’s place.

The houses I visited in England were, I thought, primitive. I remember thinking, ‘These people really live primitively compared to how we lived in Holland.’

Holland was very civilised and a well-organised country, with advanced architecture. The weighing tower in Alkmaar was built in 1554 — two hundred years before Captain Cook came to Australia.

England had very rich people — barons and earls — and then very poor people living in extreme poverty. Holland never had that same division, where people were really down and out with nowhere to live.

Of course there was poverty in Holland, but not to the extent I saw in places in England, particularly in London.

The other big difference was the education system. Children were still being caned in England and hit by teachers. That had already been outlawed in Holland for a long time.

I hardly ever saw Clifford’s father — maybe on weekends. He worked, went to the pub, came home to sleep, and then went back to work again.

The blankets were those knitted patchwork types, and I was damned cold there too. The houses were very plain — cloths like towels on the kitchen floor, hardly any heating — nothing like Dutch homes.

Actually, it reminded me of Australia when I first arrived.

I remember your mum saying, ‘Nobody has coffee tables here in Australia. There’s only a TV, a bar, or a table — nothing in the middle except liquor and a lamp with just a bulb.’

That was normal where we went. I couldn’t believe my eyes. We came from a very organised country.

In Holland, we were one of the few families with a telephone because Oma had the chemist shop in Alkmaar.

After I returned from England, I realised how advanced Holland was. In 1948 Holland had no more war rations, but England still did.

Holland got back on its feet quicker, despite German occupation and infrastructure being stripped for the war effort.

The same applied to Germany. It had been completely bombed, yet the speed of recovery was unbelievable. It was really a kind of miracle.

In 1949, while I was still in England, I met a lady called June L. She later came back to Holland with us and stayed for a fortnight.

Travelling On

The next year, in February 1950, when I was eighteen, I went to Bastilles in the north-east of France and then on to New Zealand.

I caught a boat called Sudan, chartered by a French company called Leslesingsardating.

It was a troop carrier, dropping off soldiers to various French colonies, and they filled the remaining space with migrants heading to Australia.

From Sydney, I flew to New Zealand on a large Southerland flying boat. It was a massive plane and took all night to get there.

I think it travelled at about one hundred to one hundred and ten kilometres per hour, so it took quite a while.

It had originally been a war plane, converted into a passenger aircraft.

The company was called Teal, later becoming Air New Zealand. A teal is a water bird that lives in New Zealand.

Chapter 7 — New Zealand

The Penny

When I decided to go overseas, my father gave me a 1942 shilling, a two-bob piece. I’ve still got it today.

I had started corresponding with a lady from New Zealand, Patricia W. She had sent some nice gifts to me in Holland.

Originally, my dad wanted me to go to Brazil. He said it was a big country. I had agreed for a little while, as Dad knew someone from Amsterdam who had been very successful in Brazil.

I started seriously learning Portuguese for a couple of months so I could live in Brazil and communicate. After a while, though, it just didn’t feel right to me.

I had all this stuff from New Zealand, and when push came to shove I said to my father, ‘I’m going to New Zealand.’

Just like the flip of a coin, my whole life changed by that one decision.

The Crate

I was on my way to New Zealand via Australia in January 1950. There I was at Mar Sei port, standing on the ship that was going to take me to the other side of the world — cold and miserable.

I hung overboard to watch all the people coming on board and saw my big crate still on the wharf. I could see in big letters on the crate the words ‘G. Kommer’. Granddad had organised the crate.

He put jigs for wire baskets in it, and he even put a small anvil in it. I rushed onto the gangway and said, ‘Hey, that’s my crate.’

They said, ‘Sorry sir, it’s overweight.’

I had around one hundred pounds of money from Opa and Oma, which was a lot of money in those days, and it cost me nearly sixty pounds to get that crate on board.

I carried that small anvil around New Zealand with me. I hardly ever used it. I got rid of it when I started my own business.

I really don’t know why Opa gave me an anvil. He must have thought New Zealand was very primitive.

Anyway, I had jigs for agricultural baskets, and God knows what else Opa had put in the crate. I had to get the crate onto the ship. Luckily I saw it still standing on the wharf. I can still visualise it.

Then I had to get it from Sydney to Auckland by sea, because I was travelling by Tiel airlines, which was a Southerland flying boat from Rush cutters bay to Auckland.

I actually had a big anvil sitting in the factory for a long time. A fellow business Rotarian came to visit, and they had just started the Men’s Shed.

Peter, an ex-constable in the police force, a big fellow with hands like coal shovels, was also a blacksmith and also from our wine club, the Lack-of-Nucky club. Peter saw the anvil sitting in the corner, which I had bought for around five hundred dollars at an auction, and he noticed it.

He said, ‘That’s a big anvil you have sitting in the corner. Does it sing?’

I said, ‘What do you mean?’

Apparently, when you hit an anvil it should sing and make a ping sound. I said, ‘Well, I don’t know if it sings or not, but could you use it in the men’s shed?’

So that’s where the second anvil went. They use it all the time there, and no, it doesn’t sing.

Employment in New Zealand

I worked for Mr Robertson at Avondale, then for George V on the farm, then for Alas V in the timber mills, then back to Auckland when Mum and Dad left to go back to Holland.

I worked as a maintenance engineer, then went to Christchurch where I worked as a truck driver. Then I worked for Scotts Engineering, refurbishing army trucks, then I worked for Firestone, and then I worked for myself.

Family Visit Christchurch

I had been living in New Zealand for around three years when my Oma Kommer died of breast cancer, and around seven years in New Zealand when my Opa Van der Zwad, also known as Gijs Van Der Zwad, died of bowel cancer.

I arrived in New Zealand at the end of February 1950. I was actually eighteen when I arrived in February and in March turned nineteen.

It’s a different world today. It was really like going to Mars.

Uncle Piet came out in December 1950. Opa, my dad, decided to come and say hello to us both and arrived in June 1951. Then nearly one year later, Oma (Mum) came out to New Zealand. They both went back to Holland in 1952.

Opa and I worked together for about six months in a town called Manawatu, (Whanganui) in the Kang country, on the North Island.

We had two bachelor pads, and we turned one of them into our living quarters and the other one into a sleeping quarter for us both.

We met some good friends there, Jimmy and Noel. Noel’s family took me in. They lived in Tiowamutu Tongariro. Taranaki.

I can’t trace Noel’s or Jimmy’s family in New Zealand.

Uncle Piet and I did the flip of the coin to see who would go back to Holland for our parents’ twenty-fifth wedding anniversary.

Uncle Piet said, ‘You’re the eldest, you go.’

I said to him, ‘Let’s flip the coin.’

Uncle Piet did go back to Holland and was then called up into the army.

I was called up for the ………. , which is a little like the officers’ academy. I was called up to go to the officers’ quarters when I was in New Zealand.

I then applied to the ambassador for a temporary leave so that I didn’t have to join the army, and fortunately temporary leave was granted.

That was another life-changing flip-of-the-coin moment.

I think I was quite an arrogant, snotty little fellow when I was younger in Holland. New Zealand brought me down to earth big time.

Farm Work

We had to throw the big bales of hay around on the farms when I did farm work. I remember the older men all had bad backs.

I met a lot of youngsters like myself who also did farm work. They were rugby union players, very solid, strong as an ox.

Truck Driver

I used to work as a truck driver in Christchurch. We used to pick up the empty kegs from the pubs and big heavy crates of empty bottles.

We had no power steering in those big trucks and buses. In those days females could not even turn the steering wheels to drive them around corners, so we had no female truck or bus drivers back then.

Now we have collapsible backs and the pallet jack or forklifts just go straight onto the back of the truck.

Factory Danger

The most dangerous job I ever did was at the Firestone factory in Christchurch. We used to block the automatic safety switches because they were so annoying.

The US opened the Firestone factory in 1948 and it was still very new. By 1955 it had actually produced around one million tyres. There was no occupational health and safety in those days.

What happened was the rubber had to be cured and made soft by friction. You had these massive big mills with five hundred horsepower motors to keep making the rubber softer.

As the rubber was being made softer it used to stretch out on these big rollers and then, ‘ping ping’, it would sometimes snap and come up, hitting the safety cord, and of course when the rubber hit the safety cord the mill would stop.

We used to block that safety switch so that the motors in the mill wouldn’t stop and that way you could keep on working. The supervisor would walk past, he saw the safety switch bloody blocked, and he never said anything about it.

It was unbelievable how we worked in such a dangerous place. That was stupid. I would never do that again.

Rubber stretches, you see, and before it really started warming up it would come through the mill and we used to have to constantly pick it up and bring it back onto the mill to move around. It was a hell of a job. It was practically all manual.

If it got a little bit warm it would stretch and break and then go ‘ping’, hitting the cord that would then stop the motor. We would then have to reverse the thing, because we couldn’t restart the motors without first reversing, and then we could start again.

It was a hell of a job.

To release the safety switch, you would pull this cord with a bit of a handle, and it would re-start the safety switch and then you could start the motors again.

Now it was that cord that we used to tie around somewhere so that it wouldn’t automatically switch off. That was bloody dangerous.

So when the rubber snapped, it would stay within the mill. It would hit the safety cord, but it wouldn’t stop the motor. Rubber becomes very sticky and, if you were not careful, you could have been dragged into the mill.

After a while, when the rubber became very sticky, we had to cut it out and sometimes, if your cut was wrong — oh boy — looking back at my time at Firestone, that was one of the most dangerous jobs I’ve ever had.

One of the young fellas actually got his hand mangled in the old rubber mixing mill. It was the three-roll Calendrer machine. His hand went straight through the calendrer mill.

The calendrer machine was a specialised rubber mill that used to make a very fine rubber. I used to roll the rubber up into big rolls and carry them to the extruders. That was bloody heavy.

You see, a tyre is made out of many layers of rubber. We had to feed the extruder. The extruder made inner tubes. Yeah … we did some silly things.

Motor Bike Riding Days

I had a magic motor bike in New Zealand, a brand-new Matchless twin. I’ve got this emblem patch here in the office that Peter gave to me. He bought it off eBay.

I was twenty-four in that photo, and that gun was my pea shooter, a .22, and it doesn’t shoot peas — proper bullets.

It was a small riffle, a Browning .22 semi-auto, an FN, Fabrique Nationale. It was made in an armament factory in Herstal, Belgium, a national factory of the war.

The FN riffle was not common here in Australia. That particular one had a lock where you could actually untwist the barrel out of the unit, then you had it in two pieces, and you could put both ends, each around twenty-four inches long, into your side saddle.

I also had a Lee-Enfield .303, but I only used that for deer shooting.

At that time, around 1960, we didn’t have to have a licence at all for any of our guns in New Zealand.

The FN was good for rabbits and hares. I was not a bad shot, because those hares would run fast.

We used to skin them, dissect them, and that was our dinner. Hares were running animals, so they were a bit tough to eat, so we’d soak them in a few spoons of malt vinegar for twenty-four hours.

If I was to fry it until it was brown, and the flesh was all cooked inside, it would have been dry. In order to keep it succulent, after I soaked them, I would boil the meat just a little until it was slightly cooked and then fry it in butter until it got nicely brown on the outside. Then it was very succulent and nice.

Chapter 8 — The Desire to Do Something for Myself

Self-Employment

I think my business really began with the desire to do something for myself — to do something different. That desire has to be there in the first place.

I started by making small frames for my motorcycle tank. I didn’t make much money from them, because they were made from brass wire and chrome plated. When I rode to work, I carried my lunch in it — just a small thing with little legs and suction cups underneath.

My business really started when I took my bike in for a service.

The mechanic said, ‘That’s nice — where did you get it?’

I said, ‘I made it.’

He asked how much it would cost to make some for him. I made a few, and he sold them. The problem was, I had made a Rolls-Royce for myself — top quality — so when I started producing them, they weren’t cheap.

After a while, I wanted to do some motorcycle touring, so I made frames to hold saddle bags. A friend in Christchurch, who was a leather worker, made the bags. I designed the frames specifically for my bike, using the existing bolts. They were beautiful.

When I took the bike in again, the mechanic asked, ‘Where did you get that?’

I said, ‘I made it.’

He said, ‘Could you make some for us?’

That’s what really encouraged me to start working for myself.

I did some silly things too — like carrying gas bottles on my shoulders to prove how strong I was. Some of them weighed between fifty and two hundred kilos. You’d heave them up and just carry them.

Starting Out

Uncle Peter went back to Holland, and I started working on my own in a large workshop. I sold my motorcycle and bought an oxy-acetylene set, a vice and a cutting shear — and that was it. That’s how I began working for myself.

I rented a small workshop and flat and thought, ‘What can I make?’

Father Kommer, who was still in Alkmaar, had been making foot scrapers for homes and caravans. Caravans in the 1950s were quite primitive, so I thought I’d start by making foot scrapers.

I bought flat bar steel and made around one hundred and fifty frames. They were about eighteen by eight inches, with wire tops, and I had them galvanised. They were good quality.

I worked day and night — and they didn’t sell.

People preferred the rubber ones made from old tyres. Those were cheap and everywhere.

That’s when I learned an important lesson: never make something by hand that someone else can make by machine. Always make something by hand that nobody else can make by machine.

That thinking led me into decorative wrought iron work — scrolls and detailed pieces — things that had to be made by hand.

From there, things improved. I bought a workshop with a small flat opposite Lancaster Park in Christchurch, and the business began to grow.

I sold my old 24-foot Buick and bought a 1946 Ford van. The Buick used an unbelievable amount of petrol. I was constantly topping it up just to keep it running.

The Ford van, with its small four-cylinder engine, was far better for deliveries.

Komaco

Opa’s company in Holland was called Komeco — Kommer & Company — so I named mine Komaco in New Zealand. Essentially the same idea.

Our bread-and-butter products were wrought iron items. We made thousands of small decorative pieces for walls — detailed work, all done by hand. We also used white metal.

I had a neighbour in Linwood Avenue with a terribly messy yard. One day he came over and proudly told me he’d fixed everything.

I asked, ‘What have you done?’

He said, ‘Come and have a look.’

All he had done was put up a fence. The place was still a mess.

Business Partners

Around 1957 or 1958, my partner Herman S joined me. I couldn’t do everything on my own.

Herman didn’t have any money to invest, but I gave him fifty percent of the business. He was an honest man, and we both worked very hard.

Reinforcement

I wanted to produce high-quality work, so I set up a treatment section opposite Lancaster Park. We added degreasing, chemical treatment and phosphate coating before painting — all in the one workshop.

We needed tanks for the process, which required a lot of concrete and reinforcement.

I thought, ‘What can I do with all those foot scraper frames that didn’t sell?’

They were starting to rust, so I flattened them and used them as reinforcement in the concrete. We built a large structure, and it worked very well.

Years later, in 1983, I returned with Mum and visited the old workshop. It was still there, now used by a mechanic.

One of the workers asked me, ‘Did you build that structure outside with the tanks?’

I said, ‘Yes.’

He said, ‘What on earth did you put in that concrete? It took us forever to break it up!’

I told him, ‘All those bloody foot scrapers.’

They had an enormous job removing it. That really was my piece de résistance.

First Employees

I employed a young fellow, about seventeen, earning around seven pounds a week. He stayed with me for quite a while.

Then Herman joined, and later I employed a welder and an Englishman, Tommy.

One time Tommy was sick for a couple of days, so I went to visit him at home. I had never seen such a dirty, untidy place in my life — yet they were living there quite happily.

Creative Projects

At one point, I decided to make a mahogany table for myself. The timber was expensive, and I didn’t really have the right equipment, but I had the idea.

I glued the boards together, had them planed flat, trimmed the edges, sanded everything, built the frame, and finished it properly.

By the time it was done, it certainly wasn’t cheap. It was more of an artistic project — something I felt like creating for the satisfaction of it.
