The Desire to Do Something for Myself

A Life Built Step by Step

Gijsbertus “Gil” Kommer

Recorded and edited by Robin Kommer

Preface

by Robin Kommer

Dad once said, ‘Isn’t the memory phenomenal? You close your eyes and you see yourself — in the past — there you are.’

As his daughter, I often wondered what would happen for future generations when all the fabulous stories shared and enjoyed by our family today had become long-gone memories. Our great-grandchildren, and the many generations still to come, should not miss out on the amazing tales of their Opa Kommer.

So we are documenting and recording Dad’s remarkable journey through life.

One of Dad’s memorable sayings is: ‘I’ve got no problems with that.’

I had no problems secretly recording Dad for around two years and typing up his tales. Then one day I sent him a brief sample of around ten thousand words. He read it, then quietly looked at me and remarked on my extraordinary memory in recalling his stories so well.

That gave me a laugh.

I said, ‘Dad, when was the last time you heard me tell a story in such perfect detail?’

He looked at me questioningly.

Then I produced the recordings, and for the first time he heard his own voice telling those stories. Dad sat in his chair, crossed his arms across his chest, and remained silent.

Finally, he looked at me sideways and said, ‘Well, I’d better be careful what I say to you in the future!’

It took nearly a year before Dad came to the recording party, but once he did, he became an active participant in sharing his life stories, knowing full well that I would be recording every word.

Chapter 1 — Gil’s Story

Life Is an Adventure

I’ve had so many adventures in my life and have certainly achieved a lot. I’ve also built a lot of buildings. It’s been, and still is, a very full life — with tragedies along the way.

Whether it’s losing Helen or Uncle Dick, I feel that as long as you keep living the life you’re living, you can carry them in your heart. You think of your loved ones, but you don’t allow your life to become totally different because they’re gone.

When Helen passed away, it was the saddest moment in my life. But we still had young Peter, Robbie, Bram and Annelies — I had a family. Yes, we were sad. It was a tragedy.

I still look at the photograph of Helen, and the funny thing is I’m looking at a young girl’s photograph. I’m not looking at a lady who would be fifty-two now, with a couple of kids and all.

There’s one thing I think would be silly, and that would be to have regrets — to say I would have done things differently. Regret is a very negative feeling.

If I regretted marrying the mother of my children, Ruth, then none of my children would be here — not Bram or Robbie. I wouldn’t have adopted Jennifer, and I wouldn’t have had Helen.

I can’t say that I regret. These were the decisions I made at the time. What happens when you start regretting things? Those regrets stay with you. They keep coming back into your mind.

Regrets can’t be talking points with people — you’d be bloody boring if all you spoke about was your regrets. The fact remains, if you had made one single different decision in your life, then your whole life would have been different.

Flip of a Coin

Back in New Zealand, Uncle Piet and I had to decide which one of us would go back to Holland to celebrate Oma and Opa’s twenty-fifth wedding anniversary.

At the time, Uncle Piet said, “You should go, because you’ve been here first and you’re the oldest.”

“Piet, let’s toss for it,” I replied.

We tossed the coin, and Uncle Piet went back to Holland — and his whole life changed.

My whole life would have changed if I had gone back to Holland. Look at what would have happened if I had won the toss. I would have gone back, and the army would have grabbed me. I would have stayed there, and I wouldn’t have met Ruth — your mum.

A thousand and one things could have happened. My whole life could have changed with the simple toss of a coin.

At one point, we considered moving our business to South Africa. When I saw the way South Africa was heading after the Sharpeville massacre, I decided no — South Africa was not for me.

Maybe it could have worked for me personally, but it certainly wouldn’t have worked for my children or grandchildren.

So that was another decision that changed my life — leaving New Zealand, selling up, and moving back to Holland instead of going to South Africa.

Another major decision.

When you regret a decision, you wouldn’t be in the position you are in today. You would have had a totally different life if you had taken the other path.

So there’s no use regretting anything. You can’t suddenly say, “I would have liked things to have been different,” because if things had been different at that point, your whole life would have changed.

Chapter 2 — Growing Up in the Netherlands

Family History

Granddad had two brothers and two sisters. Om Jouk had two children, Geder and Matti, who now live in America. Om Derek has one daughter left, also called Matti, who is married with children. Then there was Opa, Tante Nel, who had two children, Om Ven and Klaus, and Tante Mattie, who had three — Ewout, Marilel and Els.

So I’ve still got eight cousins.

They had another boy before Opa, and he passed away as an infant. He was also called Piet.

There was a grave for that little infant at the cemetery, and Om Jan and Opa had promised their mother — my grandmother — that they would look after the grave for as long as they lived.

At one point, the family received a notice asking what was to be done with the grave, because in Holland you have to pay a yearly fee to maintain it.

That’s how it works in Holland — it’s not like Australia.

In the end, we decided not to continue with it, as it had been a promise made by Om Jan and my father for their little brother.

Mum had a similar situation with her grandfather, Parke Jouke. Nobody liked him. Om Jouke received a phone call in Holland asking who was going to maintain that grave. He rang Mum one night and asked, “Are you going to pay for the grave?”

Mum said, “Not me — I never liked him.”

He replied, “Good on you, I didn’t like him either.”

What happens then is that the grave is eventually cleared. The remains are removed, cremated, and the name disappears.

There is, however, a heritage grave in a very old part of town for Mum’s grandmother. She lived to 103 and is buried on church grounds with her husband. That land is not council ground.

In Holland, names carry through. A woman keeps her maiden name attached to her married name.

Our Kommer family in Holland traces back to Berkhout. In Alkmaar there was Om Jan, Geder and Matti. We were born on the Hofplein. In Broek op Langedijk was Om Derek, and in Heerhugowaard lived Tante Nel and Tante Mattie for a time.

The Beginning

I was born Gijsbertus Kommer on 28 March 1931 to my parents, Joanne Helen Kommer and Peter Kommer.

I was one of four sons — the eldest, Bram Kommer, then Peter Kommer, myself, and Derek Kommer.

Town Life

It’s always interesting talking about the history of a town, and how much things have changed in the local villages over time.

Back then, towns had many tradespeople. Basket and cane weavers were very common. We had the baker and the cobbler. Steel wasn’t widely used for everyday purposes, so the cobblers and blacksmiths worked on all the equipment for horses and carts.

There was a fellow who sold knives and scissors, but he also sharpened them. He had a specialty shop for sharpening shears used for sheep. Another trade was the wool comber at the shearing sheds.

The kaasboer — the cheese maker — lived only a few houses down from us in Alkmaar, opposite the Waagtoren church. He made a type of cheese similar to what you’d call Kraft cheese today.

One tradition that has lasted the test of time is the brass band. I think every village has one. Alkmaar actually had four — the city brass band, and even the Catholics had their own.

Bolsward, where Mum lived, was a small city of around ten thousand people, and it had three brass bands.

Dutch lollies were an absolute favourite in our family. As kids, we loved a “magic ball”, which we could buy for one cent. It changed colours as you sucked it, which is why we called it magic.

Another was the dauwdrop, a very soft liquorice. You pressed it with your thumb, and then you had this lump of liquorice sitting on your thumb. So we’d walk around sucking our thumbs with it — a bit stupid, really.

Chapter 3 — Family Life

Waking Up with Ice Sheets

Waking up with ice on our blankets was a regular thing. The vapour from our breath would freeze on top of the blankets. It could be ten to fifteen degrees of frost outside, and we were sleeping upstairs with absolutely no heating.

The bedrooms would eventually reach extreme cold, yet we were still warm in bed under thick woollen blankets. There was no central heating in those days. Even the water in the sink would have ice on it.

We kept a small pot under the bed so we didn’t have to go downstairs in the middle of the night in the freezing cold.

We were on a sewage system with a large tank in the backyard, similar to a bio-cycle system. Every couple of years, a special truck would come and empty it.

Farmers had outhouses with just a simple hole. Not like the Australian ones with deep drops — just a basic setup.

Homemade Underwear

Oma knitted underwear for all the children, along with the high woollen socks we wore while walking or riding our bikes to school in the snow.

My Parents

My father was born in 1906. He left school at the age of twelve, in 1918, just after the First World War. He was a hard worker.

At thirteen, he left home to work on a local dairy farm. That was quite normal in those days — once you finished primary school, you went out to work.

Dairy farming is hard work, seven days a week, starting very early each morning. It was often easier to live with the farmer and his family.

My grandparents lived in a small house in Ber…, and Piet P, the farmer, lived about half a kilometre up the road. So my father was still close to home.

Piet became a lifelong friend of Dad’s. He had a daughter named Nel. Later, after Piet passed away, Nel married a doctor, Hank K., and they lived in Bereka near Alkmaar. The friendship between the families continued for many years.

Dad developed a strong interest in cattle and built a close friendship with a local vet, who trained him as a veterinary midwife. He helped cows during difficult births and, when necessary, saved the cow if a calf had died inside.

It was tough work, but cattle were a livelihood. If you had thirty or forty cows in those days, you were considered a wealthy farmer. You also had horses, pigs, and calves for the following year — everything needed to be housed through winter.

Winters back then were much harsher than they are today.

My mother was also born in 1906. She studied at college and became a schoolteacher in a small town north of Bravaand, called Peel.

That’s where Mum and Dad met. Their relationship began in Peel, and they were married there on 6 June 1929.

While travelling between farms to help with cattle, Dad realised there was a shortage of simple medicines and ointments for animals.

At the time, medicines could not easily be registered unless developed with proper pharmaceutical training. The nearest college teaching pharmacy was in the city of Horan, about twenty-three kilometres from Alkmaar.

Mum enrolled there and rode her pushbike five days a week through all kinds of weather to study. She even continued cycling while pregnant with her fourth son, Oom Derek. With a strong headwind, you’d be lucky to manage five kilometres an hour. The bikes had no gears.

Dad was also travelling constantly by pushbike, building his business among the farmers.

We were looked after by a housemaid, a local girl named Cret, who was about seventeen at the time. To us, she seemed quite old. It was common for farmers’ daughters to take live-in domestic work so they could earn a wage and have somewhere to live.

Cret stayed with us until she married. We also knew her father well — a cheerful man with a big head of white curly hair and one lazy eye. He used to make flutes or whistles for us out of long reeds.

Mum’s pharmacy business in Alkmaar developed many medicines, particularly for farm animals, including a black drawing ointment.

Over time, Mum and Dad built up the business. They had agents in England, Berlin, and Copenhagen, and developed a small factory called Komico (Kommer & Company), producing medicines for livestock.

Our bathroom even became a small laboratory where Mum created her formulas. Everything was professionally packaged and sold through agents. She never patented her formulas.

Mum later gave me a book containing all her recipes and discoveries. I never lost it — I just don’t know where it is at the moment.

Dad was a bold businessman, despite having very little formal education.

At one stage, a serious chicken disease spread across farms, killing thousands. A research laboratory owned by Jacquar in Genbloux in Belgium developed a vaccine.

Three companies in Holland competed to market it. Dad’s business was the smallest. They were given a few months to prove who could develop the market the fastest.

Dad went all out. His advertising was extensive — newspapers, posters — he was brilliant. He won the contract ahead of the larger companies.

It was a major turning point in the business.

Later, there were issues with their London agency, and money stopped coming back to Holland. Dad sent the accountant, Klaus De F., to investigate.

He arrived in London around 8 May 1940. Two days later, Germany invaded Holland. He didn’t return for five years.

The London office was eventually bombed, and Dad never recovered the money.

By the end of the war, the business had collapsed, and he had to start again from nothing.

But Dad had energy and a strong entrepreneurial mind. Even in retirement in Australia, he was always developing new ideas — from Van Diemen’s Gifts at the factory to planting garlic.

The company Komico still exists in Holland today, producing some of the original ointments Mum developed.

My Grandparents

On Dad’s side, both his parents were orphans.

Both my grandfathers — from Mum’s and Dad’s sides — were house painters.

When insurance became more common, allowing people to insure farms, homes, and livestock, my grandfather Opa Kommer took over an insurance agency and became a well-known agent in the local town.

By then, my father’s parents had moved to Alkmaar. I visited them often as a child. They lived only about two hundred metres from our home.

My mother’s parents lived in Varaswen. Opa and Oma Van der Zwaard — about eighty kilometres from Alkmaar.

Chapter 4 — Youthful Adventures

Falling into the Canal

When I was young, a big part of my life was living opposite the Alkmaar Kaasmarkt (cheese market) and the big church, the Waagtoren, on the Waagplein. I lived there from about four to fifteen or sixteen, and left the area straight after the war.

I was about four or five years old when I fell into the canal near our home in Alkmaar. People later told me they saw my clogs floating. I was just a child riding my scooter around. I wanted to make a big right-hand turn towards home, and as I turned, I rode straight into the canal.

They told me the clogs came up first because they were timber. I remember sinking under the water, and when I opened my eyes I saw all this green water. Obviously, I held my breath, and then I sort of floated to the top. Luckily, I surfaced behind one of the boats and not underneath it. I hung onto the rudder of the boat.

When I looked up, I saw all these stupid faces looking at me — all the faces of the farmers.

At the time, a Dutchman called Jan Vas Spadol, who was studying for the priesthood, lived in the house across the street from the canal. He happened to look out of his second-floor window at the very moment I fell in.

Apparently, he rushed down the stairs, across the road, climbed onto the boat, and pulled me up out of the canal by the arm. I didn’t like that move much because it hurt. He just heaved me up and out of the water, like that.

I went into our house and Mum — your grandmother — was shocked to pieces. She dried me off while I stood in the kitchen.

I can clearly remember crying and asking Mum, ‘Will I die now?’

That’s when Mum decided, ‘That’s it, you are all taking swimming lessons.’ So the three of us — Peter, Bram and I — all went off to swimming lessons.

I was about seven years old when we had to do our swimming test. The water was very clear, and they had a wooden body at the bottom of the pool. You had to dive into the deep end and bring the wooden body up from the bottom to the top. Then you had to swim with one arm, holding its chin with the other arm, and get the wooden body to the side of the pool. There were really two parts to the lessons — one was to swim, and the other was rescue.

School

You were not allowed to use the cane when I was growing up. It had already gone in most parts of Holland. I did see one boy hit with a ruler. He had to open his hand, but it was more token discipline than anything else.

Your mum, who lived in Friesland, saw boys being hit at school with a big wooden stick — at least a metre long and quite thick. They got hit on the backside and had to bend over. Some of them were big boys, sixteen or eighteen years old.

I’m naturally left-handed, but I had to write with my right hand. Your mum’s father was the same. Whenever I picked up the pen with my left hand, the teacher would quietly move it to my right. In the early days, being left-handed was seen as not good, or a little bit evil.

I left school when I was seventeen or eighteen. I was very good at arithmetic. We had to do complex sums — no calculators back then. My weakness was the French language.

Dance Classes

My teacher was Ms Prada. Mum wanted us to socialise more, as we were four boys with no sisters, so she organised dance classes with Tante Janny B, who was a seamstress.

There was the four of us. Bram danced with Janny B, and I danced with Neil DV. She was a friend of Janny’s and the daughter of a butcher.

I could never dance the waltz. I was always getting my feet mixed up when I tried to turn around. Bloody awful dance for me. That was the first dance we were taught, and I still can’t do the waltz.

Ice Hopping

We did some dangerous things in our youth. The winters were very severe back then — a lot more severe than they are now. Icebreakers would go through the canals, breaking up the ice so ships could get through. What they left behind were all these big floating blocks of ice.

We used to do what we called ‘Scotch-a-lopen’, a bit like hopscotch, jumping from one block of ice to another. Scotch-a-lopen translated roughly meant a scotch being a block of ice, and lopen meaning walking.

When you stood on a smaller one, it went down under your feet and you had to quickly jump to the next ice block. We did stupid things. We did daft things. If you fell into the icy water, you were in trouble, because you couldn’t lift yourself back onto the ice. People, including kids, had fallen in and had to be rescued.

Some of the ice blocks were so small they sank as soon as you stepped on them, so you had to jump quickly to the next one, otherwise your weight would take you under. You were aiming for the big blocks first, so you’d go bop-bop-bop, and then you’d be cheering.

I think it was in about 1944. There were about four of us doing it when all of a sudden there was this yell from the bank. One of the fathers had seen his son Scotch-a-lopen.

The boy was standing on this big block of ice, like the three of us, and he was scared to go back because he knew he was going to get a hiding.

His father was shouting, ‘Come back here, you! How dare you do that!’

Half the time, parents didn’t even know what their kids were up to.

Ice Skating Dangerously

The most dangerous thing I ever did on skates — I can still see it now — was on the mead. We had very simple ice skates. You had to make certain the steel of the skate was ground in a particular way for a left and a right foot. It wasn’t just a bit of steel.

What happens when you’re ice skating is that you are turning, using your ankles, and pushing yourself off the ice to propel forward. The blade had to be cut at a slight angle to create that edge which made proper contact with the ice.

I was never a very good ice skater because I had problems with my ankles. They were weak and got tired. I really liked ice skating, though, and felt jealous of the people who could skate around for hours. I would have liked to skate until the sun went down.

There was a big bridge with arches underneath it, where the water would flow through. In the very severe winter of 1944–1945, the ice in the centre under that bridge had actually frozen over.

The dare was to skate fast and get enough speed, then get down on your hunches, because you couldn’t stand, and carry through in that tunnel to the other side.

Whilst you were going through under the bridge, you could hear the ice moving around you. It wasn’t quite cracking, but it was shifting. That was one of the silliest things I ever did, though luckily a lot of us got through it.

It was thin ice, not thick ice, and you could hear it making this high-pitched sound like ‘eeeeeee’. You kept your eyes fixed on the opening at the other side. We had to get a hell of a speed up, then sit on our hunches and try to get through because you couldn’t stand under the bridge — it was only about ... high.

I never went back through from where I started.

Elfstedentocht, Weather and Winter Memories

The Elfstedentocht happened nearly every year when I was young. That was the big ice-skating competition with eleven cities in Friesland, and they would skate for almost two hundred kilometres through those eleven cities. The ice had to be at least fifteen centimetres thick throughout the whole course for the race to happen, and it hasn’t happened now for many years.

I think climate change is a furphy. Climate change is being observed, from what I’ve heard, on all the planets in our solar system. They are all having climate change at this very moment. Every 280 years in history, the sun goes into a quieter cycle. The sun is now starting that hibernation cycle. They’ve found what they call climate change or global warming is being observed on all the planets — Venus, Mars, Jupiter — all of them. It’s got nothing to do with earth alone. It’s about sunspots and that sort of thing. They don’t tell you about that. It doesn’t suit them to tell you about that. This is when we get a mini ice age, and we’re at the start of that now.

In London, the smog was huge. There was that famous London smog where you couldn’t see your own hand, so they banned coal fires. In Holland, coal was used to heat the homes. Anthracite was a good quality coal from Maastricht. It was a very fine coal with the highest carbon content and very few impurities. Anthracite would keep the stove going day and night, and that would keep the house warm.

A lot of farmhouses had cooking utensils where you put the pot on over kerosene and a wick. You’d walk into the house and smell cooking and kerosene.

Stealing Cheese from the Cheese Market

When we were younger, our family lived opposite the cheese market in Alkmaar. When all the farmers, each with their own specialty cheese, had set themselves up at the market, the buyers would core the cheese for tasting.

When they weren’t looking, my brothers and I, or some of my friends, would sneak in, pull out the core piece of cheese, and put the little waxed cork top back in. We had fun.

All the buyers would have this cheese core, which they used to taste the quality of the cheese. It was a genuine market at the time. The farmers would come to town every second or third month with their horse and cart. A farmer well known for his premium-quality farmer’s cheese, or Boarer kaas, might have saved up, say, thirty cheeses.

He would have his little place at the market, and his clientele would sample the cheese by coring through the protective wax, tasting a bit, then placing the core back so it didn’t look full of holes from the outside.

By the 1920s and 1930s, big milk and cheese factories had started up in Holland. These factories became bigger and bigger. The small family co-ops of perhaps ten farmers in a district would combine to get enough milk to produce their own quality cheese. The flavour and quality were often well-kept family secrets, because that was their livelihood and reputation.

It wasn’t long before many of the smaller family businesses started to disappear because of the competition from the big factories.

Today, those factories are enormous. Holland is one of the biggest cheese exporters in the world. If you go to a large cheese stall in a local market in Holland, there might be a hundred and fifty different types of cheese. You can still ask for a special Boarer kaas.

Chad actually found a speciality cheese shop in Sydney and bought me some cheese.

Chad said, ‘Have a taste of this, Opa.’

I said, ‘Well, that’s one of the best cheeses I’ve tasted.’

Chad just loved that cheese.

Nature Bath

The regional archives of Holland show so much of Dutch history. It really is like looking into the past. One photograph in particular was of the Vantesiebad, Carbatong, an outdoor pool — a sort of nature bath.

It was not unlike a swimming pool, but it was set in nature, and this one was made in 1917. It is so far gone in my memory now, looking back into the past like that.

It’s all far more sophisticated now than when I was there. There were all these little buildings where people got changed. There was a little point, warmed by the sun, between the shallow and the deep end. It was a wonderful nature bath, opening on the first of May for the summer.

We used to swim in very cold water. I was born in ’31, so it’s all gone now. It’s just a park with a pond and ducks swimming in it. It became no longer suitable for swimming because of regulations about water quality.

Your mum would agree that, back in those days, canals ran through all our villages and we all just swam in them. Even today, when we go to Holland and hire a boat for our holidays, kids are still swimming in the canals.

The water in the canals is very hygienic now. They’ve cleaned it up so much you can just swim in it. They’ve also put restrictions on farms and what fertilisers can go into the ground.

Chapter 5 — Second World War

German Occupation

During the war, our way of life was surrounded by rumours. The liveliest part of every conversation was always the rumours.

We managed to procure a radio, which was illegal during the war. It was a new radio back in 1938–1939, a German Blaupunkt. As a matter of fact, I think Uncle Bram still has that radio.

It was an excellent radio for receiving shortwave broadcasts from England. So, right throughout the war, we were able to listen to the secret BBC radio. If we had been discovered, there would have been a massive penalty. Not even our neighbours or friends knew we owned it.

The Germans controlled the news through a cable network system. Anyone with a radio capable of receiving shortwave had to hand it in to be destroyed. The average worker in Holland couldn’t afford a radio anyway and relied on the cable system, which only had three stations. That was how the Germans controlled the information.

I remember listening to the BBC and hearing about the first real defeat of the Germans. American troops had joined the Australians, English and Polish, under General Montgomery, and defeated the Germans at El Alamein.

I remember Mum being so incredibly happy.

She said, ‘Those so-and-so’s have been defeated!’

During the war, Opa provided food and dairy for our family by breeding rabbits and keeping cows. There was a small piece of land — a polder just outside Alkmaar — called the Huisvaderpolder.

Opa had become friendly with the farmers in that area. One of them was Piet H. and his German wife, whom we called Muider Malansha, a bit like Marlene Dietrich.

Dad rented a small piece of land from Piet H. and kept a couple of cows there. Later, when Dad had a factory in Alkmaar, he employed Piet H., who worked for him until he retired. Dad was very loyal to people who were loyal to him.

We had a fairly hard taskmaster in Father Kommer. He wasn’t brutal or cruel, but he made us work. He made us understand that it was our responsibility. It had nothing to do with child labour — everyone had to pull their weight for the family.

Hiding Dad in the Attic

We lived in a house that was probably built around the 1570s, around the time the Waagtoren had been completed in 1574. The city was designed around the church, with all the small streets.

It was an old home that had been rebuilt and altered over hundreds of years. There were areas within the house that had been blocked up during renovations. We found a solid space with a trapdoor hidden in a cupboard above some shelves. You could climb up, lift the hatch, and crawl into a decent-sized hiding space. There was another space on the second floor, big enough to hide a few people.

We didn’t use these hiding places until we knew for certain there would be raids, as they were quite claustrophobic. Often someone would warn us that a German raid — a Razzia — was coming.

One of the local blacksmith’s sons was in the Hitler Youth and fighting at the front. He sometimes passed on information about when raids were likely to happen.

Dad made his own salamis and sausages, and when he hid, he would quietly prepare food. At one point he had even procured a bottle of gin. During one raid, he sat there making sausages and having a drink. At one stage he even started singing, and Mum had to warn him to be quiet.

Hiding Boys from the Germans

Mum and Dad hid a few boys during the war to protect them from being taken by the Germans — a bit like the story of Anne Frank.

We hid Piet V and another young Dutchman. To keep them occupied, Dad gave them odd jobs. One job was chopping the roots of massive trees into kindling. That was incredibly hard work, splitting roots with wedges and hammers.

At least it kept them busy. We didn’t have that many raids, but during the last two years of the war, the underground resistance became more active. That’s when the Germans started picking up young men and even shooting some involved in the resistance.

Many young men between eighteen and thirty-three were taken to Germany as forced labour. By 1944, the German war industry was at its peak, and a large percentage of workers were foreign labourers from occupied countries.

A friend of mine was taken to Germany. He worked there for a short time before the factory was bombed. He escaped with his uncle, who later died in a bombing raid. My friend experienced around twenty bombing raids while in hiding.

When he was finally free, there were no trains or buses. It took him three months to walk back to Holland. His family had no idea whether he was alive or dead.

Blackouts

I remember a knock at our door one day while we were listening to the radio. I was about twelve years old, and we panicked. Mum was working in the pharmacy, and through the glass door we saw a man in uniform.

Someone quickly grabbed the radio and hid it. An ashtray sitting on top of it fell and clattered down the stairs, making a lot of noise — which we absolutely didn’t want.

It turned out to be a Dutch policeman, telling us he could see light coming through our windows. As soon as the sun went down, blackout regulations were strictly enforced. No light could be visible, otherwise aircraft could spot targets. It was total darkness every night.

Towards the end of the war, we had no electricity and relied on candles and kerosene lamps. We never had any problems with water.

War Reflections

There were many stories about the war — about operations, battles and turning points. One that stands out is Operation Market Garden, where airborne troops were dropped into Arnhem to try to secure key bridges.

The Germans had strong forces there, including tank divisions, and the operation ultimately failed. The war was turning, but it was far from over.

Hitler had already suffered major defeats in Russia, including Moscow and Stalingrad, but he refused to surrender. His decisions led to enormous loss of life, including millions of Jews and the destruction of much of Europe.

Even when ordered to destroy Paris, the German commander refused, allowing the city to be liberated.

The scale of loss was enormous. Russia alone lost tens of millions of people during the war.

Youthful Earning Adventures

Even during those years, I was a bit of an entrepreneur. At around thirteen, I started selling tobacco.

My brothers and I all had savings accounts at the Nutspaarbank. Opa used to divide his cash between our accounts, partly to avoid tax.

At one stage, I had seven or eight thousand guilders in my account. Later, after the war, heavy taxation measures were introduced to eliminate black-market money, and Opa had to withdraw it all.

After that, I was back to having just a few guilders again.

Chapter 6 — Post War

Jubilation

The real sense of liberation came when the Americans entered Paris. The jubilation of the people was incredible. They suddenly knew they were free — the tyranny was gone. It was like a massive spring that had been held under tension throughout the war, and all at once it was released.

When the Canadians came rumbling through Alkmaar, you knew you were free. The joy and dancing lasted for three full days. It never stopped, and there was no liquor — it was just pure, unadulterated joy. No more soldiers on the streets with guns. The tyranny was gone.

When the Americans entered Rome, Italy surrendered. All of a sudden, the Italians became enemies of the Germans, whereas before they had been allies.

In Alkmaar we had the White Cross, the Red Cross and the Green Cross. The White Cross was mainly a disinfectant station. Before the war, many people had problems with lice and similar conditions, so they disinfected both people and clothing.

They had large steam units where blankets and clothes were treated. At that time, a large number of Italians were coming through. Some wore those distinctive alpine hats.

A friend of mine, whose father managed one of the White Cross stations, told me that many of these Italians were prisoners of war. The Germans had to process them through the disinfectant stations first. Their clothes were taken, they were washed and issued new clothing, and everything was steamed. That’s how bad the lice problem was.

England, 1948

After the war, in 1948, I went to England for a couple of weeks. I was only seventeen. It was during the summer holidays, around June or July. I took a boat from IJmuiden to Harwich.

I have never been so cold in all my life. It was an old passenger boat, and I found a spot underneath a staircase. There was a coil of rope there, and I sat on it, miserable and freezing. I remember thinking, ‘I’ll just sit here until we bloody arrive.’

The journey took about four to five hours. From Harwich, I went on to Bath and stayed with Clifford W.

Coming from Holland, which had endured five years of German occupation, I was surprised by how primitive some of the homes in England seemed. I thought to myself, ‘These people live very simply compared to us.’

Holland was a very organised and advanced country. The Waagtoren in Alkmaar had been built in 1554 — two hundred years before Captain Cook arrived in Australia.

England had extremes of wealth. There were very rich people — barons and earls — and then very poor people living in real poverty. Holland didn’t have that same level of divide. There was poverty, but not like what I saw in parts of London.

The education system was also different. Children were still being caned in England. That had already been abolished in Holland.

I hardly saw Clifford’s father. He worked, went to the pub, came home to sleep, and then went back to work again. That seemed to be his routine.

The houses were very plain. The blankets were those knitted patchwork types, and I was cold there too. There were cloths on the kitchen floors, hardly any heating — nothing like Dutch homes.

It reminded me a bit of Australia when I first arrived. I remember your mum saying, ‘Nobody has coffee tables here. Just a TV, a bar, or a table — and maybe a lamp.’

That was normal in Australia at the time. Coming from Holland, which was very organised, it was quite a contrast. We were even one of the few families with a telephone, because Oma had the chemist shop.

After returning from England, I realised how advanced Holland was. By 1948, Holland no longer had rationing, but England still did. Holland recovered faster, even though we had been occupied and had infrastructure stripped during the war.

Germany was another example. The country had been completely bombed, yet the speed of its recovery was unbelievable. It really was a kind of miracle.

In 1949, while I was still in England, I met a lady called June L. She later came back to Holland with us and stayed for a fortnight.

Leaving for New Zealand

The following year, in February 1950, when I was eighteen, I travelled to Bastia in the north-east of France, and from there set off for New Zealand.

I caught a boat called the Sudan, chartered by a French company, Messageries Maritimes. It was a troop carrier, transporting soldiers to French colonies, but it had spare space which was filled with migrants heading to Australia.

From Sydney, I flew to New Zealand on a large Sunderland flying boat — a massive aircraft. It took all night to get there. I think we travelled at about one hundred kilometres per hour, so it took quite a while.

It had originally been a war plane, converted into a passenger aircraft. The airline was called TEAL, which later became Air New Zealand. A teal is a water bird native to New Zealand.

Chapter 7 — New Zealand

The Penny

When I decided to go overseas, my father gave me a 1942 shilling — a two-bob piece. I’ve still got it today.

I had started corresponding with a lady from New Zealand, Patricia W, who had sent me some nice gifts in Holland.

Originally, Dad wanted me to go to Brazil. He said it was a big country, and he knew someone from Amsterdam who had been very successful there. I agreed for a while and even started learning Portuguese for a couple of months so I could live there and communicate.

After a while, though, it just didn’t feel right. I had all this connection with New Zealand, and when push came to shove, I said to my father, ‘I’m going to New Zealand.’

Just like the flip of a coin — my whole life changed with that one decision.

The Crate

I was on my way to New Zealand via Australia in January 1950. I was standing at Marseille port, on the ship that was going to take me to the other side of the world — cold and miserable.

I leaned over the side of the ship to watch people boarding and saw my big crate still sitting on the wharf. In big letters it said, ‘G. Kommer.’ Granddad had organised it.

He had packed jigs for wire baskets, and even a small anvil. I rushed down the gangway and said, ‘Hey, that’s my crate!’

They told me it was overweight. I had about one hundred pounds from Opa and Oma — a lot of money in those days — and it cost me nearly sixty pounds just to get that crate on board.

I carried that small anvil all over New Zealand. I hardly ever used it. I eventually got rid of it when I started my own business. I don’t really know why Opa gave it to me — he must have thought New Zealand was very primitive.

I also had to get the crate from Sydney to Auckland by sea, because I travelled by TEAL airlines — a Sunderland flying boat — from Rushcutters Bay to Auckland.

Later on, I had another anvil in my factory. A fellow Rotarian, Peter — a former police constable and a blacksmith — saw it and asked, ‘Does it sing?’

Apparently, a good anvil makes a ringing sound when struck. I told him I didn’t know, but he could take it to the men’s shed. They still use it there — and no, it doesn’t sing.

Employment in New Zealand

I worked in a number of jobs across New Zealand. I worked for Mr Robertson at Avondale, then on farms, then in timber mills, and later back in Auckland as a maintenance engineer.

I moved to Christchurch, where I worked as a truck driver, then for Scotts Engineering refurbishing army trucks, then for Firestone, and eventually I went into business for myself.

Family and Early Years

I had been in New Zealand for about three years when my Oma Kommer died of breast cancer, and about seven years when my Opa Van der Zwaard — also known as Gijs — died of bowel cancer.

I arrived in New Zealand at the end of February 1950, aged eighteen, and turned nineteen the following month. It was a completely different world — like going to Mars.

Uncle Piet arrived in December 1950. Opa (Dad) came out in June 1951 to visit us both, and about a year later Oma followed. They returned to Holland in 1952.

Opa and I worked together for about six months in Manawatu, on the North Island. We had two bachelor huts — one for living and one for sleeping.

We made some good friends there, Jimmy and Noel. Noel’s family took me in at one stage. I’ve never been able to trace them since.

Uncle Piet and I once flipped a coin to decide who would go back to Holland for our parents’ twenty-fifth wedding anniversary. He said I should go because I was the eldest, but I suggested we toss for it.

Piet lost the toss, went back, and ended up being called into the army. I was later called up as well, but I applied through the ambassador for a temporary exemption and was granted leave.

That was another life-changing moment — just like the flip of a coin.

I think I was a bit of an arrogant, snotty young fellow in Holland. New Zealand brought me down to earth very quickly.

Farm Work

On the farms, we had to throw around big bales of hay. I remember the older men all had bad backs from that work.

Many of the younger fellows were rugby players — strong as oxen. It was hard, physical work.

Truck Driving

I worked as a truck driver in Christchurch, collecting empty kegs from pubs and heavy crates of bottles.

Those trucks had no power steering. In those days, it took real strength just to turn the wheel. Everything was manual — very different from today.

Factory Danger

The most dangerous job I ever had was at the Firestone factory in Christchurch. There was no occupational health and safety in those days.

We used to block the automatic safety switches because they kept stopping the machines. Massive rubber mills, driven by powerful motors, would stretch the rubber. When it snapped, it would hit the safety cord and shut everything down.

To keep working, we would tie the safety cord so the machines wouldn’t stop. That was extremely dangerous. If the rubber caught you, it could drag you straight into the mill.

One young fellow actually had his hand mangled in the rolling machine. Looking back, it was one of the most dangerous environments I’ve ever worked in. We did some very silly things.

Motorbike Days

I had a beautiful motorbike in New Zealand — a brand-new Matchless twin.

I also had a .22 rifle, a Browning FN, which I used for rabbits and hares. At that time, around 1960, we didn’t need a licence for firearms in New Zealand.

We would hunt hares, skin them, and cook them. Because hare meat is tough, we soaked it in malt vinegar for a day, then lightly boiled it before frying it in butter. That kept it tender and quite good to eat.

It was a different life altogether.

Chapter 8 — The Desire to Do Something for Myself

Self-Employment

I think my business really began with the desire to do something for myself — to do something different. That desire has to be there in the first place.

I started by making small frames for my motorcycle tank. I didn’t make much money from them, because they were made from brass wire and chrome plated. When I rode to work, I carried my lunch in it — just a small thing with little legs and suction cups underneath.

My business really started when I took my bike in for a service.

The mechanic said, ‘That’s nice — where did you get it?’

I said, ‘I made it.’

He asked how much it would cost to make some for him. I made a few, and he sold them. The problem was, I had made a Rolls-Royce for myself — top quality — so when I started producing them, they weren’t cheap.

After a while, I wanted to do some motorcycle touring, so I made frames to hold saddle bags. A friend in Christchurch, who was a leather worker, made the bags. I designed the frames specifically for my bike, using the existing bolts. They were beautiful.

When I took the bike in again, the mechanic asked, ‘Where did you get that?’

I said, ‘I made it.’

He said, ‘Could you make some for us?’

That’s what really encouraged me to start working for myself.

I did some silly things too — like carrying gas bottles on my shoulders to prove how strong I was. Some of them weighed between fifty and two hundred kilos. You’d heave them up and just carry them.

Starting Out

Uncle Peter went back to Holland, and I started working on my own in a large workshop. I sold my motorcycle and bought an oxy-acetylene set, a vice and a cutting shear — and that was it. That’s how I began working for myself.

I rented a small workshop and flat and thought, ‘What can I make?’

Father Kommer, who was still in Alkmaar, had been making foot scrapers for homes and caravans. Caravans in the 1950s were quite primitive, so I thought I’d start by making foot scrapers.

I bought flat bar steel and made around one hundred and fifty frames. They were about eighteen by eight inches, with wire tops, and I had them galvanised. They were good quality.

I worked day and night — and they didn’t sell.

People preferred the rubber ones made from old tyres. Those were cheap and everywhere.

That’s when I learned an important lesson: never make something by hand that someone else can make by machine. Always make something by hand that nobody else can make by machine.

That thinking led me into decorative wrought iron work — scrolls and detailed pieces — things that had to be made by hand.

From there, things improved. I bought a workshop with a small flat opposite Lancaster Park in Christchurch, and the business began to grow.

I sold my old 24-foot Buick and bought a 1946 Ford van. The Buick used an unbelievable amount of petrol. I was constantly topping it up just to keep it running.

The Ford van, with its small four-cylinder engine, was far better for deliveries.

Komaco

Opa’s company in Holland was called Komeco — Kommer & Company — so I named mine Komaco in New Zealand. Essentially the same idea.

Our bread-and-butter products were wrought iron items. We made thousands of small decorative pieces for walls — detailed work, all done by hand. We also used white metal.

I had a neighbour in Linwood Avenue with a terribly messy yard. One day he came over and proudly told me he’d fixed everything.

I asked, ‘What have you done?’

He said, ‘Come and have a look.’

All he had done was put up a fence. The place was still a mess.

Business Partners

Around 1957 or 1958, my partner Herman S joined me. I couldn’t do everything on my own.

Herman didn’t have any money to invest, but I gave him fifty percent of the business. He was an honest man, and we both worked very hard.

Reinforcement

I wanted to produce high-quality work, so I set up a treatment section opposite Lancaster Park. We added degreasing, chemical treatment and phosphate coating before painting — all in the one workshop.

We needed tanks for the process, which required a lot of concrete and reinforcement.

I thought, ‘What can I do with all those foot scraper frames that didn’t sell?’

They were starting to rust, so I flattened them and used them as reinforcement in the concrete. We built a large structure, and it worked very well.

Years later, in 1983, I returned with Mum and visited the old workshop. It was still there, now used by a mechanic.

One of the workers asked me, ‘Did you build that structure outside with the tanks?’

I said, ‘Yes.’

He said, ‘What on earth did you put in that concrete? It took us forever to break it up!’

I told him, ‘All those bloody foot scrapers.’

They had an enormous job removing it. That really was my piece de résistance.

First Employees

I employed a young fellow, about seventeen, earning around seven pounds a week. He stayed with me for quite a while.

Then Herman joined, and later I employed a welder and an Englishman, Tommy.

One time Tommy was sick for a couple of days, so I went to visit him at home. I had never seen such a dirty, untidy place in my life — yet they were living there quite happily.

Creative Projects

At one point, I decided to make a mahogany table for myself. The timber was expensive, and I didn’t really have the right equipment, but I had the idea.

I glued the boards together, had them planed flat, trimmed the edges, sanded everything, built the frame, and finished it properly.

By the time it was done, it certainly wasn’t cheap. It was more of an artistic project — something I felt like creating for the satisfaction of it.
